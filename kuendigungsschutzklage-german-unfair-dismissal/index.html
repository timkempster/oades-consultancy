Kündigungsschutzklage: How to Challenge an Unfair Dismissal in Germany

A plain-English guide to Germany's unfair dismissal claim – the three-week deadline, how the courts work, what it costs, and what you can realistically expect.

If you have been dismissed from a job in Germany and believe the dismissal was unjustified, German law gives you a powerful tool: the Kündigungsschutzklage. This is a formal legal action filed in the German labour court (Arbeitsgericht) asking a judge to declare that your dismissal was invalid and that your employment relationship continues.

The process is well-established and widely used in Germany, but it comes with strict rules – most critically, a three-week deadline that cannot be extended if missed. This guide explains what the Kündigungsschutzklage involves, who can bring one, how the process works, and what outcomes are realistic.

Critical deadline: Under § 4 KSchG (Kündigungsschutzgesetz), you have exactly three weeks from receiving your written dismissal notice to file a claim at the labour court. If you miss this deadline, the dismissal becomes legally binding regardless of how unjustified it was – there is almost no route back.

What is a Kündigungsschutzklage?

A Kündigungsschutzklage is a declaratory action – you are asking the court to declare that your employment relationship was not lawfully terminated. It is not primarily a claim for compensation (as UK employment tribunal claims typically are), but a challenge to the validity of the dismissal itself.

The standard legal petition reads, in effect: "It is declared that the employment relationship between the parties was not terminated by the employer's notice of dismissal dated [date]."

If the court agrees, your employment legally continues from the date of the original dismissal – and your employer owes you all wages for the intervening period. In practice, however, the vast majority of cases settle before a final judgment, typically with a severance payment and agreed termination.

Who can bring a claim?

Anyone in an employment relationship at the time of dismissal can file a Kündigungsschutzklage. However, the level of protection available depends on two factors:

  • Length of service: The general protection of the Kündigungsschutzgesetz (KSchG) only applies after six months of continuous employment with the same employer. Before that point, employers can dismiss employees without giving a reason, and the Kündigungsschutzklage has limited practical impact (though it can still be brought).
  • Size of the employer: The KSchG's general protection applies only in companies with more than ten employees. In smaller businesses, employers have wider freedom to dismiss, though other legal grounds may still apply.

Additional protections apply to certain categories of employee regardless of company size or tenure – including pregnant employees, employees on parental leave, severely disabled employees, and members of a works council (Betriebsrat). These groups require regulatory approval before dismissal and have stronger legal protections.

The three-week deadline (§ 4 KSchG)

This is the most important rule in German dismissal law. Under § 4 of the Kündigungsschutzgesetz, as confirmed by the official German statutory text at gesetze-im-internet.de, an employee who wishes to challenge a dismissal must file a claim at the Arbeitsgericht within three weeks of receiving the written notice of dismissal.

The consequences of missing this deadline are severe: under § 7 KSchG, the dismissal is deemed effective from the outset. Once the three weeks have passed without a claim being filed, it is no longer possible to challenge the dismissal in court on any grounds – not on procedural grounds, not on the basis of social justification, not on any basis at all.

A few points to note about the deadline:

  • The clock starts when you receive the written notice, not when it is sent. Dismissals must be in writing under § 623 BGB – a verbal dismissal has no formal legal effect and the three-week clock does not start running.
  • If the dismissal requires prior regulatory approval (for example, in the case of severely disabled employees), the three-week period begins only once the relevant authority's decision is communicated to the employee.
  • If your employer sends a second, subsequent dismissal notice while your original claim is pending, each new dismissal requires its own separate challenge within three weeks.

Grounds for challenging a dismissal

German law requires that dismissals of employees covered by the KSchG be sozial gerechtfertigt – socially justified. A dismissal is only justified on one of three grounds:

  • Personal reasons (personenbedingte Kündigung): Relating to the employee's personal circumstances, such as long-term illness that makes continued employment impossible.
  • Conduct reasons (verhaltensbedingte Kündigung): Based on a breach of contractual duties by the employee. In most cases, a prior formal warning (Abmahnung) is required before a conduct dismissal will be upheld.
  • Operational reasons (betriebsbedingte Kündigung): Due to genuine redundancy – a reduction in the employer's workforce requirements. The employer must show that the position is no longer needed and that the correct redundancy selection criteria were applied.

If a dismissal does not fall clearly into one of these categories, or if procedural requirements were not followed (such as consulting a works council where one exists), the dismissal is likely to be found invalid.

How the court process works

German labour court proceedings are structured differently from most other civil litigation. The process typically unfolds in two stages:

  1. Conciliation hearing (Güteverhandlung): This is scheduled within approximately two weeks of the claim being filed. It is presided over by a single judge and its purpose is to explore settlement. The judge will typically give an early indication of the strengths and weaknesses of both sides. The majority of Kündigungsschutzklage cases are resolved at this stage, with the parties agreeing a termination date and severance payment.
  2. Main hearing (Kammertermin): If no settlement is reached at the conciliation hearing, the case proceeds to a full hearing before a three-person chamber (one professional judge and two lay assessors representing employers and employees respectively). This typically takes place three to five months after the conciliation hearing. Evidence is presented and the court delivers a judgment.

Judgments can be appealed to the regional labour court (Landesarbeitsgericht), and in some cases to the Federal Labour Court (Bundesarbeitsgericht) on points of law.

Costs

One distinctive feature of German first-instance labour court proceedings is the costs rule: neither party can recover their legal costs from the other, regardless of who wins. Each side bears its own lawyers' fees. This is different from most civil litigation in Germany and from UK employment tribunal practice.

Court fees in first-instance labour proceedings are modest and are frequently waived if the case settles at the conciliation hearing. Legal fees are calculated based on the value of the dispute, which is typically set at one quarter of the employee's annual gross salary.

The practical effect of this costs rule is that it lowers the financial risk of bringing a claim, but also means that even a fully successful employee does not recover their legal costs. For employees with limited resources, legal aid (Prozesskostenhilfe) may be available.

Typical outcomes: severance and settlement

In practice, the large majority of Kündigungsschutzklage cases do not result in a final judgment. They are resolved by settlement (Vergleich), typically at the conciliation hearing. The typical settlement involves:

  • The employee formally accepting the termination of employment
  • The employer paying a severance sum (Abfindung)
  • Agreement on a favourable employment reference (Arbeitszeugnis)
  • Clarification of any outstanding wage claims

There is no statutory formula for severance in dismissal cases (unlike, for example, UK statutory redundancy pay). However, a widely used informal benchmark is half a month's gross salary per year of service. The actual amount is negotiable and depends heavily on the legal strength of the employer's position, the employee's prospects of re-employment, and the parties' respective willingness to settle.

Where a court does find a dismissal invalid but decides that reinstatement is not viable – for example because the working relationship has broken down irreparably – it can order the dissolution of the employment relationship with a compulsory severance payment (Auflösungsantrag). This is relatively rare in practice.

When does a claim make sense?

A Kündigungsschutzklage tends to be most worthwhile when:

  • The dismissal is legally questionable – for example, no prior Abmahnung was given before a conduct dismissal, or the redundancy selection was not carried out correctly
  • The employee has been employed for a significant period and stands to receive meaningful severance
  • The employee is unlikely to find equivalent re-employment quickly
  • The dismissal was made without following the required works council consultation procedure
  • The employee belongs to a protected category (pregnancy, parental leave, disability, works council membership)

It is less likely to achieve a favourable outcome where the dismissal was clearly justified on conduct grounds, where the employee has only recently started work, or where the company employs ten or fewer people.

What about employees based outside Germany?

If you worked for a German employer – whether based in Germany or abroad – and were dismissed, the Kündigungsschutzgesetz may still apply to your employment, depending on the governing law of your contract and the circumstances of your work. The three-week deadline applies regardless of where you are based when you receive the dismissal notice.
