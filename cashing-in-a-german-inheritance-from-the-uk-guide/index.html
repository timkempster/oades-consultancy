How to cash in a German inheritance from the UK (or abroad): step-by-step

A complete guide to identifying, proving and receiving German inheritance assets from outside Germany.

If you have inherited assets in Germany, Oades Consultancy can guide you through identifying, proving and ultimately receiving those assets. The process is formal and document-driven, but predictable when handled correctly.

Each case is fact-specific; however, the following outlines the typical steps in a cross-border German inheritance.

10 Steps to Receiving Your German Inheritance from the UK (or abroad)

1

Obtain the death certificate (Sterbeurkunde)

Request the official death certificate from the local registry office (Standesamt) where the death was registered. Multiple certified copies are usually required for banks, courts and authorities.

2

Secure and review any will

If a will exists, it must be submitted to the competent probate court (Nachlassgericht). The court will formally open the will and issue an opening record (Eröffnungsprotokoll).

3

Establish proof of inheritance

You must be able to prove your legal status as heir. With a notarised will, the will together with the court opening record may suffice, provided the chain of inheritance is clearly established. Without a will, or if the will is unclear, an Erbschein (certificate of inheritance) must be obtained from the Nachlassgericht.

4

Identify the estate assets

Determine what assets exist in Germany. Typical categories include bank accounts, real estate, securities and investments, insurance policies, tax refunds and more. This step often requires contacting institutions and, where necessary, requesting information as a potential heir.

At Oades Consultancy, we can assist in identifying and locating estate assets in Germany from the UK (or abroad), including where these are not yet fully known.

5

Arrange payment logistics

Heirs should ensure they can receive euro payments efficiently before requesting any payouts. In practice, this usually involves holding a euro-denominated account capable of receiving SEPA transfers. Services such as Wise or Revolut are commonly used for this purpose. This helps avoid unnecessary currency conversion and non-SEPA transaction fees, which can otherwise accumulate significantly.

6

Address inheritance tax obligations

German inheritance tax is administered by the Finanzamt responsible for the deceased's last residence. Heirs are generally required to notify the tax office of the inheritance (unless the matter is already fully known to the authorities via official channels). The Finanzamt will decide whether to request a formal inheritance tax return (Erbschaftsteuererklärung).

7

Satisfy institutional requirements and request payout

Before releasing funds, German banks and other institutions typically require:

  • – Proof of inheritance (Erbschein or will + opening record)
  • – Death certificate
  • – Valid identification of the heirs
  • – Payment instructions (typically signed by all co-heirs unless representation is established)

Whilst not permitted by law, in practice banks and institutions generally require an Unbedenklichkeitsbescheinigung before releasing or transferring assets. This is a confirmation from the Finanzamt that inheritance tax matters have been addressed.

8

File the inheritance tax return (Erbschaftsteuererklärung) if requested

If required by the Finanzamt, a formal inheritance tax return must be submitted, including full disclosure of the estate and applicable allowances.

9

Receive and review the tax assessment (Steuerbescheid)

The tax office will issue a formal assessment setting out any tax due. If correct, this should be paid within the stated deadline. If incorrect, an objection (Einspruch) may be filed.

If inherited real estate is later sold, separate income tax considerations (e.g. speculation tax under §23 EStG) may arise.

10

Distribution of the estate

Once heirship is established and the tax position is sufficiently clarified, assets are released and distributed among the heirs in accordance with their respective shares.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to travel to Germany to claim an inheritance?
No, a German inheritance can be handled entirely remotely. Documents that would ordinarily require a German notary can be executed abroad using apostille services, allowing the process to be managed efficiently from the UK or elsewhere.
How long does a German inheritance process take?
In straightforward cases, the process typically takes 4–8 months. Timeframes depend on whether an Erbschein is required, the complexity of the estate (particularly real estate) and processing times at the relevant authorities, including the Nachlassgericht and Finanzamt. Contested or complex estates will take longer.
What is an Erbschein and when is it required?
An Erbschein is a certificate of inheritance issued by the Nachlassgericht and serves as formal proof of heirship under German law. It is typically required where there is no will, where the will is not notarised, or where banks and institutions require formal proof beyond a will and its opening record.
How much does an Erbschein cost?
Fees are set by statute under the Gerichts- und Notarkostengesetz (GNotKG) and are calculated based on the value of the estate. As a general guideline, costs are approximately 0.3%–0.4% of the estate value. For an estate of €500,000, fees are typically in the region of €1,000–€2,000.
Will I pay inheritance tax in both Germany and the UK?
Germany and the UK do not have a specific double taxation treaty for inheritance tax. However, relief may be available under UK law for foreign tax paid on foreign assets. The position can vary significantly depending on residency, asset type, and structure, so case-specific advice is recommended. Tax on gains from German real estate is generally covered by the UK–Germany Double Taxation Agreement.
What are the German inheritance tax allowances?
Allowances depend on the relationship to the deceased:
  • Spouses: €500,000
  • Children: €400,000
  • Grandchildren: €200,000
  • Other heirs: significantly lower
Tax rates range from 7% to 50%, depending on both the value of the inheritance and the degree of kinship.
Do I need a German bank account to receive funds?
No. However, a euro-denominated account capable of receiving SEPA transfers is strongly recommended to ensure efficient payment and minimise banking fees.
Can I refuse a German inheritance?
Yes. A refusal (Ausschlagung) must be declared to the Nachlassgericht within 6 weeks of becoming aware of the inheritance, or 6 months if the heir resides outside of Germany. Failure to act within this period results in deemed acceptance – including if inheriting debt.
What happens if there are multiple heirs?
Where there is more than one heir, they form an Erbengemeinschaft (community of heirs). This means:
  • Estate assets are jointly owned
  • Decisions must generally be taken collectively
  • Distribution occurs only once the estate is formally divided
What if I have been disinherited?
German law provides close relatives with a statutory entitlement (Pflichtteil) even where they have been excluded from a will. This reflects the strong protection of family inheritance rights under the Constitution.
What if I do not know the full extent of the estate?
Heirs have legal rights to request information from banks, insurers, and other institutions. Oades Consultancy has particular experience in identifying and recovering previously unknown or inaccessible assets within Germany.
Do UK grants of probate work in Germany?
No. UK grants of probate are not recognised as proof of inheritance in Germany. German institutions require German documentation, typically an Erbschein or a notarised will together with its official opening record.
Can UK documents be used in German inheritance proceedings?
UK documents (such as birth, marriage, and death certificates) will generally require notarised and apostillised copies – often with a certified German translation. We at Oades Consultancy are happy to arrange these procedures without you needing to travel far.
