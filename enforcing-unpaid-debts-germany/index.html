How to Collect and Enforce an Unpaid Debt in Germany

A plain-English guide to recovering money owed by a debtor in Germany – from the first demand letter through the Mahnverfahren, Vollstreckungsbescheid and Zwangsvollstreckung to cross-border enforcement.

If someone in Germany owes you money – whether you are a business owed payment for goods or services, an individual owed a loan, or a UK-based creditor with a German debtor – German law provides a structured and well-established route for recovering that debt. The process can be pursued largely in writing, without a full civil trial in many cases, and without necessarily needing to be physically present in Germany.

This guide explains the full pathway: from the first formal demand through the German court payment order procedure (Mahnverfahren), to obtaining an enforceable judgment (Vollstreckungsbescheid), and ultimately compelling payment through forced enforcement (Zwangsvollstreckung). It also covers the European Payment Order procedure, which is particularly useful for cross-border debt recovery within the EU.

Limitation period: Under § 195 BGB, most money claims in Germany become statute-barred after three years from the end of the calendar year in which they arose. A debt that arose in 2022 expires at the end of 2025. Acting promptly – and interrupting the limitation period by filing a claim – is essential.

Step 1: Send a formal payment demand (Mahnung)

Before taking any court action, the standard first step is to send a formal written demand letter (Mahnung). Although a creditor is not always legally required to send a Mahnung before commencing court proceedings, doing so serves several important purposes:

  • It formally puts the debtor in default (Verzug) under § 286 of the German Civil Code (Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch, BGB). Default triggers the creditor's entitlement to statutory late-payment interest.
  • It creates a clear written record that the debt was demanded and refused or ignored, strengthening your position in any subsequent proceedings.
  • It gives the debtor one final opportunity to pay, which often results in voluntary payment without the cost of further action.
  • It demonstrates good faith, which courts and arbitrators consider relevant in any subsequent dispute over costs.

The demand should be sent in writing (post or email, though registered post is preferable for evidential reasons), specify the amount owed, set a clear payment deadline (typically 10–14 days), and state that legal proceedings will follow if payment is not received. It is not necessary to use any specific form of words, but clarity is important.

Late-payment interest: what the law entitles you to

Once a debtor is in Verzug (default), German law automatically entitles you to interest on the outstanding amount. The rate is set by § 288 BGB:

  • Consumer transactions: the base interest rate (Basiszinssatz) set by the European Central Bank, plus 5 percentage points.
  • Business-to-business transactions: the base rate plus 9 percentage points.

The Basiszinssatz is adjusted twice per year (1 January and 1 July) by the Deutsche Bundesbank and published in the Federal Gazette. As of early 2025, the base rate has been positive following successive ECB rate rises, making total B2B late-payment interest over 12% per annum. You can check the current rate on the Bundesbank's official website.

In B2B transactions, you are also entitled to a flat-rate compensation fee of €40 per overdue invoice under § 288(5) BGB, which was introduced to implement the EU Late Payment Directive (Directive 2011/7/EU). This does not require a separate demand.

Step 2: The Mahnverfahren – Germany's court payment order procedure

If the formal demand goes unanswered or is refused, the most efficient route to a court-backed order in Germany is the Mahnverfahren – a summary written procedure governed by §§ 688–703d of the German Code of Civil Procedure (Zivilprozessordnung, ZPO). It is designed for undisputed or uncontested money claims and does not require a full hearing before a judge.

The procedure operates as follows:

1
File the application for a payment order (Mahnbescheid) The creditor submits a standardised application to the competent Mahngericht (the local court at the creditor's domicile or, for online applications, a centralised court). The application can be filed online via the official portal online-mahnantrag.de, maintained by German state justice ministries. No supporting documents are submitted at this stage – only the claim details and the parties' particulars.
2
Court issues the Mahnbescheid (payment order) Provided the application is formally complete, the court will issue the Mahnbescheid and serve it on the debtor. The court does not examine the merits of the claim at this stage – it simply checks the formal requirements. The process typically takes a few days to two weeks.
3
Debtor's options: pay, object or ignore Once served, the debtor has two weeks to respond. They can pay the full amount claimed, lodge a formal objection (Widerspruch), or do nothing. If they pay, the matter ends. If they object, the case is referred to the ordinary civil court for full proceedings. If they do nothing, the creditor can proceed to the next step.
4
Apply for an enforcement order (Vollstreckungsbescheid) If no objection is raised within the two-week window, the creditor applies to the court for a Vollstreckungsbescheid – an enforcement order. This application must be made within six months of the Mahnbescheid being issued. The court issues the enforcement order, which has the same legal force as a judgment after a full civil trial.
5
Debtor has two further weeks to object (Einspruch) The debtor receives the Vollstreckungsbescheid and has a further two weeks to lodge an objection (Einspruch). If they do, the case is again transferred to the ordinary civil court. If they do not, the Vollstreckungsbescheid becomes final and enforceable.
6
Enforcement (Zwangsvollstreckung) With a final Vollstreckungsbescheid (or a court judgment following contested proceedings), the creditor can enforce the order against the debtor's assets – wages, bank accounts, moveable property or real estate.

What happens if the debtor objects?

If the debtor lodges a Widerspruch against the Mahnbescheid, or an Einspruch against the Vollstreckungsbescheid, the summary procedure ends and the case is transferred to the competent civil court for ordinary proceedings. The creditor will need to substantiate the claim with evidence, and the case proceeds as a standard civil action (ordentliches Klageverfahren).

This is not necessarily a setback – an objection simply means the debtor is contesting the claim. If the claim is well-founded and supported by evidence (invoices, contracts, correspondence, loan agreements), the civil court will likely rule in the creditor's favour. The key difference is that ordinary proceedings take longer (typically six to twelve months for a first-instance judgment) and involve higher legal costs.

In contested proceedings, the usual German civil costs rule applies: the losing party pays the winning party's costs, including legal fees and court fees. This is a significant difference from English litigation practice and can make pursuing a justified claim financially worthwhile.

Zwangsvollstreckung: enforcing a judgment in Germany

Once you hold a final and enforceable title – whether a Vollstreckungsbescheid, a civil court judgment, or a notarial enforcement title – you can commence Zwangsvollstreckung (compulsory enforcement) against the debtor's assets. Enforcement is governed by §§ 704–945 ZPO. The main enforcement mechanisms are:

Wage and salary garnishment (Lohnpfändung)

The creditor applies to the enforcement court for a garnishment order (Pfändungs- und Überweisungsbeschluss, PfÜB) directed at the debtor's employer. The employer is ordered to pay a portion of the debtor's net wage directly to the creditor. The amount that can be garnished is subject to statutory protected minimums (Pfändungsfreigrenzen), which are set out in § 850c ZPO and updated annually – in 2024, the basic protected amount is approximately €1,409 per month. Anything above the protected threshold can be seized.

Bank account garnishment (Kontopfändung)

A PfÜB can equally be directed at the debtor's bank. The bank is ordered to freeze and transfer funds in the debtor's account to the creditor, up to the amount owed. Debtors are entitled to a basic protected balance under the Pfändungsschutzkonto (P-Konto) rules – currently approximately €1,409 per month in 2024 – which cannot be seized. Amounts above that threshold, and any additional deposits, can be garnished. If you do not know which bank the debtor uses, there are procedures to obtain this information.

Seizure of moveable assets (Sachpfändung)

A court-appointed enforcement officer (Gerichtsvollzieher) visits the debtor's address and seizes moveable property of value for public auction. This mechanism is often less effective in practice for consumer debtors, as many valuable items are either exempt from seizure or jointly owned, but it can be useful against business debtors.

Real estate enforcement (Immobiliarvollstreckung)

Where the debtor owns real estate in Germany, a creditor can apply for a Zwangshypothek (enforcement mortgage) to be registered on the land register, or apply for forced auction (Zwangsversteigerung) or forced administration (Zwangsverwaltung) of the property. These routes are slower and more complex but can be appropriate for larger debts.

Information about assets (Vermögensauskunft)

If you do not know what assets the debtor has, you can request that the enforcement court require the debtor to provide a sworn statement of their assets (Vermögensauskunft) under § 802c ZPO. Debtors who refuse or lie face serious legal consequences. This sworn declaration is entered into a central register (Schuldnerverzeichnis) and is accessible to creditors.

Cross-border debt recovery: the European Payment Order

For creditors based outside Germany – in the UK, the EU, or elsewhere – who are owed money by a debtor in Germany, there is an additional route available for cross-border claims within the EU: the European Order for Payment (Europäischer Zahlungsbefehl), established by Regulation (EC) No 1896/2006.

The European Payment Order (EPO) procedure works in a broadly similar way to the German Mahnverfahren:

  • The creditor files a standardised application (Form A) with the competent court in the member state where the debtor is domiciled – in Germany, this is a dedicated central court (AG Wedding in Berlin for most cases).
  • The court issues a European Payment Order without examining the merits, which is then served on the debtor.
  • The debtor has 30 days to lodge an objection (Widerspruch). If they do not object, the EPO becomes automatically enforceable in all EU member states without further formality.
  • If the debtor objects, the matter proceeds to ordinary civil proceedings in Germany.

The EPO is particularly useful because a final EPO obtained in any EU member state – including Germany – is enforceable across the EU without the need for a further declaration of enforceability. This has significant practical value for UK creditors pursuing debts within the EU's single enforcement area.

Post-Brexit note: Following the UK's departure from the EU, UK courts can no longer issue European Payment Orders and UK judgments are no longer automatically enforceable within the EU under the Brussels I Recast Regulation. However, a European Payment Order issued by a German court at the request of a UK-based creditor remains fully valid and enforceable across EU member states. UK creditors can still use the EPO procedure by directing their application to the German court.

Limitation periods: do not wait too long

German law imposes strict limitation periods on debt claims. Under § 195 BGB, the standard limitation period for most contractual money claims is three years. The clock starts running at the end of the calendar year in which the claim arose and the creditor became aware of the debtor's identity and the circumstances giving rise to the claim.

In practice, this means:

  • A debt that arose (and was known to the creditor) in the calendar year 2022 expires at midnight on 31 December 2025.
  • A debt arising in 2023 expires at the end of 2026.
  • Special limitation periods apply in some cases – for example, claims arising from construction contracts, or claims relating to defective goods under the sale of goods rules, may have different periods.

The limitation period is interrupted (Hemmung or Unterbrechung) by certain acts, including filing a Mahnbescheid application, issuing proceedings, or the debtor making an acknowledgement of the debt. Once interrupted, a fresh three-year period begins. This makes it critical to take formal action before the deadline expires – not merely to send letters.

Costs of the Mahnverfahren and civil proceedings

The Mahnverfahren is considerably cheaper than full civil proceedings. Court fees are calculated under the Court and Notary Fees Act (Gerichts- und Notarkostengesetz, GNotKG) and the Court Costs Act (Gerichtskostengesetz, GKG). For a €5,000 claim, court fees for the Mahnbescheid stage are typically around €87.50 (at 0.5 times the standard fee unit). For comparison, the same claim taken to full civil proceedings would incur fees of approximately €525 in first-instance court costs alone.

If the case proceeds to contested civil proceedings and you win, the debtor is ordered to reimburse your court fees and your reasonable legal costs (calculated under the Rechtsanwaltsvergütungsgesetz, RVG). German civil litigation therefore has a fundamentally different risk profile from UK litigation: the loser pays, which strengthens the case for asserting a legitimate, well-evidenced claim.

Practical considerations for UK-based and international creditors

If you are based in the UK or outside Germany, there are some additional practicalities to be aware of when pursuing a German debtor:

  • Language: German court proceedings and documents are conducted in German. Applications to the Mahngericht and enforcement courts must be in German. Working with an adviser fluent in German legal procedure is important.
  • Service of documents: Service of the Mahnbescheid on a debtor within Germany is handled by the German court automatically. No additional steps are required from the creditor for domestic service.
  • Authorised representatives: While legal representation is not mandatory in first-instance proceedings before the Amtsgericht (local court), it is required before the Landgericht (regional court, for claims above €5,000) and above. For any contested matter of substance, professional representation in Germany is strongly advisable.
  • Enforcing a UK judgment in Germany: UK judgments obtained after Brexit are no longer automatically enforceable in Germany. A creditor with a UK judgment wishing to enforce against German assets must generally commence fresh proceedings in Germany (Klage auf Feststellung der Vollstreckbarkeit), using the UK judgment as evidence of the underlying debt. This is a more burdensome route and reinforces the value of pursuing a fresh German claim where the debtor is German-domiciled.
  • Currency: German courts deal in euros. If your debt is denominated in another currency, the conversion rate and any exchange-rate arguments will need to be addressed in your claim.

When does formal debt enforcement make sense?

Not every unpaid debt justifies the cost and effort of formal legal action. The following factors suggest that pursuing enforcement is worthwhile:

  • The debt is clearly evidenced in writing – by an invoice, contract, acknowledgement, loan agreement, or email correspondence
  • The amount is significant enough that the costs of the Mahnverfahren and any subsequent enforcement are proportionate
  • The debtor has identifiable assets or income in Germany against which enforcement can be directed
  • The debtor is in default – that is, they have received a demand and either refused to pay or ignored it
  • The limitation period has not yet expired

It is worth noting that in the majority of Mahnverfahren applications, the debtor does not object – they either pay upon receipt of the Mahnbescheid, or they allow the Vollstreckungsbescheid to become final. The formal pressure of a court-issued payment order is often enough to prompt voluntary payment from a debtor who simply needed a sufficiently serious prompt.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use the German Mahnverfahren if I am based in the UK?
Yes. A creditor based in the UK (or elsewhere outside Germany) can file a Mahnbescheid application against a German-domiciled debtor. The application can be submitted online via the German portal. Jurisdiction typically follows the debtor's domicile, and the competent court will be located in Germany. The application itself must be in German.
Does the debt have to be in euros to use the Mahnverfahren?
Under § 688(1) ZPO, the Mahnverfahren is available only for claims for payment of a definite sum of money. Claims in foreign currencies are not excluded in principle, but in practice applications are processed in euros and the claim amount must be precisely quantified. Claims denominated in other currencies should generally be converted to euros using the applicable exchange rate at the time of application.
What if I don't know the debtor's exact address?
An exact address is required to serve the Mahnbescheid. In Germany, address information can sometimes be obtained from the official residents' registration system (Einwohnermeldeamt), which allows certain third-party enquiries. For business debtors, the commercial register (Handelsregister) is publicly accessible and provides the registered office address. Where the debtor cannot be located, alternative service mechanisms exist but are more complex.
How long does the Mahnverfahren take?
If the debtor does not object, the process from filing to obtaining a final Vollstreckungsbescheid typically takes six to ten weeks. The initial Mahnbescheid is usually issued within one to two weeks of a complete application being filed; the debtor then has two weeks to object; the creditor must then apply for the Vollstreckungsbescheid (typically another two weeks); and the debtor has a further two weeks to lodge an Einspruch. If all stages pass without objection, you will have an enforceable order in approximately six to eight weeks from application.
What is the difference between a Mahnbescheid and a Vollstreckungsbescheid?
A Mahnbescheid is a payment order – it tells the debtor they have been claimed against and gives them two weeks to pay or object. It is not yet enforceable. A Vollstreckungsbescheid is an enforcement order – issued once the debtor has not objected to the Mahnbescheid, it has the same effect as a court judgment and authorises compulsory enforcement against the debtor's assets.
Can I recover my legal costs from the debtor?
In civil proceedings (contested matters before an ordinary court), Germany operates a "loser pays" rule: the court will order the losing party to reimburse the winner's court fees and statutory legal costs. In the Mahnverfahren, the costs of the application are included in the order itself, so a successful creditor recovers the application fee from the debtor as part of the enforcement. However, if no court proceedings are taken, the costs of simply sending a demand letter are generally not automatically recoverable without a specific contractual or statutory basis.
What happens if the debtor is insolvent?
If the debtor is already insolvent – or becomes insolvent after you obtain your order – enforcement may be ineffective in practice. An Insolvenzantrag (insolvency petition) filed against the debtor will result in an Insolvenzverfahren (insolvency proceedings), during which individual enforcement is automatically stayed. Creditors must register their claims in the insolvency schedule. The likelihood and level of recovery depends on whether there are sufficient assets in the estate. Before committing to enforcement costs, it is worth checking whether insolvency proceedings are already pending at the competent Insolvenzgericht (insolvency court).
Does the three-year limitation period apply to all debts?
The three-year standard period under § 195 BGB applies to most contractual and quasi-contractual claims. However, some claims have different limitation periods: for example, claims arising from a notarially recorded acknowledgement of debt (notarielles Schuldanerkenntnis) or a Vollstreckungsbescheid itself are subject to a 30-year limitation period under § 197 BGB. This is why it is important to obtain a court-backed title even if immediate enforcement is not possible – the title will remain valid for decades, giving you the ability to enforce if the debtor's circumstances improve.
