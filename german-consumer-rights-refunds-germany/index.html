Refunds, Exchanges & Consumer Protection Rights in Germany

A plain-English guide to Mängelgewährleistungsrechte – your statutory rights when goods you buy in Germany turn out to be defective, including a worked example involving a high-value imported car.

Germany has some of the strongest consumer protection laws in the world. When you buy goods – whether in a shop, online, or from a dealer – and those goods turn out to be defective, German law gives you four distinct remedies to choose from, backed by a statutory two-year warranty period. These rights are known collectively as Mängelgewährleistungsrechte (defect warranty rights) and are codified in §§ 434–445 of the German Civil Code (Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch, BGB).

For English speakers buying high-value goods in Germany – whether a luxury car, a piece of industrial machinery, or an expensive appliance – understanding these rights is essential. They apply whether you are based in Germany or abroad, and they cannot be waived or reduced by a seller in a consumer transaction. This guide explains exactly what you are entitled to, when you must act, and how to pursue a claim effectively.

Key principle: Under § 434 BGB, a seller must deliver goods that are free from material defects and free from defects of title. If they do not, the buyer acquires a menu of statutory remedies – regardless of what any small print or sales contract may say. In consumer transactions, these rights cannot be contractually reduced below the statutory minimum.

What counts as a defect under German law?

A Sachmangel (material defect) under § 434 BGB exists when the goods do not conform to what was agreed in the contract, or – where no specific quality was agreed – when they are not fit for their ordinary purpose or do not meet the standard a reasonable buyer would expect. In practice, this covers:

  • Manufacturing faults or flaws in materials present at the time of delivery
  • Goods that do not work as described, advertised, or demonstrated
  • Goods delivered in the wrong quantity, or a different variant to the one ordered
  • Goods that were assembled incorrectly by the seller (or their agent)
  • Hidden defects that only become apparent after some use – for example, a mechanical fault in a vehicle that develops within the warranty period
  • Goods that do not come with the accessories, documentation, or installation instructions that were promised or reasonably expected

Critically, the defect must have been present at the time of delivery, even if it does not manifest immediately. German law creates a significant evidential presumption in the buyer's favour for the first twelve months after purchase (see the section on burden of proof below).

The four statutory remedies (Mängelrechte)

Once a defect is established, § 437 BGB gives the buyer access to four remedies. The first – subsequent performance – must generally be attempted before the others become available. If it fails or is refused, the buyer may move to any of the remaining three.

Remedy 1

Subsequent Performance

Nacherfüllung — § 439 BGB

The buyer demands either repair of the defect or delivery of a replacement, defect-free item. The choice is the buyer's. The seller bears all costs: transport, labour, materials, and any necessary dismantling and reinstallation. The seller may refuse the chosen method only if it would cause disproportionate costs. A seller is generally entitled to two attempts at repair before the buyer can invoke the stronger remedies.

Remedy 2

Withdrawal from Contract

Rücktritt — §§ 440, 323 BGB

If subsequent performance fails, is refused, or is unreasonable to wait for, the buyer may withdraw from the contract entirely and demand a full refund of the purchase price. The seller must also bear the costs of returning the goods. Minor defects do not generally entitle the buyer to withdraw – the defect must be more than negligible in scope.

Remedy 3

Price Reduction

Minderung — § 441 BGB

As an alternative to full withdrawal, the buyer may choose to keep the goods but claim a proportionate reduction in the purchase price. The reduction is calculated by comparing the value of the defect-free goods with the value of the goods in their defective state at the time of sale. This is a useful remedy where the buyer still wants the item but was overcharged for its actual condition.

Remedy 4

Damages & Compensation

Schadensersatz — §§ 440, 280, 281 BGB

Where the seller is at fault (or is deemed to be at fault), the buyer can claim financial compensation for losses caused by the defect – for example, the cost of renting a replacement vehicle while the defective car is being repaired, or consequential financial losses. Damages can be claimed alongside or instead of the other remedies, depending on the circumstances.

Warranty period and burden of proof

The statutory warranty period under § 438 BGB is two years from the date the goods are delivered to the buyer. This applies to new goods sold by a trader to a consumer. For used goods, the seller may contractually reduce this period to one year – but only in consumer transactions concluded prior to 1 January 2022; for newer used-goods sales, the minimum is still one year by express agreement, and silence means the full two years applies.

Statutory warranty period
2 years

from date of delivery for new goods sold by a trader (§ 438 BGB). Cannot be reduced in consumer contracts.

Buyer-favourable presumption
12 months

Within the first year, the law presumes the defect pre-existed delivery. The seller must prove otherwise (§ 477 BGB).

The burden of proof rules under § 477 BGB are particularly important:

  • Within the first 12 months: If a defect appears, the law presumes it was present at the time of delivery. The seller must prove the defect arose afterwards (for example, through misuse by the buyer). This is a strong statutory presumption in the buyer's favour.
  • Between 12 and 24 months: The presumption reverses. The buyer must now demonstrate that the defect was present at the time of delivery – for example, through expert reports, contemporaneous documentation, or manufacturer records.

This means that buyers who discover a defect quickly – and document it promptly – are in a much stronger legal position than those who wait. Acting within the first twelve months is almost always advantageous.

Worked example: buying a car from Germany and finding it defective

Case Study

The €28,000 car that arrived with a hidden transmission fault

James, based in the UK, travels to Munich and purchases a three-year-old BMW 5 Series from an authorised BMW dealership for €28,000. He receives a signed purchase contract, an invoice, and the vehicle's full service history. James drives the car to Calais and ships it to the UK, completing the importation formalities. Total cost including shipping, import duty, and VAT adjustment: approximately €31,500.

Six weeks after delivery, a persistent judder appears when changing gear under load. An independent BMW specialist in the UK diagnoses the problem: the mechatronic unit in the eight-speed automatic transmission is failing – a known fault on this model generation. The repair estimate is €3,200. The specialist confirms the fault would have been developing for months and was present at the time James purchased the car.

James contacts the Munich dealership. They initially offer to "take a look" if he brings the car back to Germany. James knows his rights and pushes for more. Here is how German law responds to each option he has.

Option A: Demand repair at the dealer's cost (Nacherfüllung)

Because the fault appeared within six weeks of delivery – well within the first twelve months – the law presumes the defect was present at delivery. James does not need to prove this; the dealer must prove the fault arose from misuse. Since James can demonstrate normal use and the specialist has confirmed the fault was pre-existing, the dealer cannot rebut the presumption.

James is entitled to demand that the dealer repairs the transmission at no cost to him under § 439 BGB. Importantly, the dealer must bear all costs associated with the repair – and this includes costs that arise because James is now based in the UK. Under § 475(6) BGB (as amended by the EU Sale of Goods Directive 2019/771, implemented into German law in 2022), the seller bears transport and similar costs even where the buyer has moved the goods to a different location after delivery, provided this is reasonable in the circumstances. A specialist BMW dealership dealing with a regular cross-border sale to a UK buyer should reasonably anticipate this.

In practice, James has two main choices: either arrange for the car to be assessed by a German-approved BMW workshop in the UK (with costs reimbursed by the dealer), or return the car to the Munich dealer. If the dealer refuses either option unreasonably, James can move to the next remedies.

Option B: Withdraw from the contract and claim a full refund (Rücktritt)

If the Munich dealer refuses to cooperate, or if their repair attempt fails (or they attempt more than twice and the fault persists), James may withdraw from the entire purchase contract under §§ 440 and 323 BGB. Withdrawal means James is entitled to:

  • A full refund of the purchase price (€28,000)
  • Return of any associated costs, to the extent these flow from the defect – the dealer cannot simply ignore transport costs James incurred as a direct consequence of the seller's non-conforming delivery
  • The seller bears the cost of the car being returned to them

A defective transmission on a car worth €28,000 is plainly not a "minor defect" – it directly impairs the core function of the vehicle and is expensive to repair. The threshold for withdrawal is satisfied. James would send a formal written notice of withdrawal (Rücktrittserklärung) to the dealer, setting a reasonable deadline (typically 14 days) to agree on the refund mechanism and logistics.

Option C: Keep the car and claim a price reduction (Minderung)

If James decides he wants to keep the car – perhaps he has already spent money on UK registration and insurance and does not want the disruption of returning it – he can instead claim a price reduction under § 441 BGB. The reduction is proportionate to the defect. In this case, a €3,200 repair cost on a €28,000 vehicle is roughly an 11% reduction. James would be entitled to approximately €3,200 back from the purchase price, calculated by reference to the difference in value between what he agreed to buy and what he actually received.

Option D: Claim damages for consequential losses (Schadensersatz)

On top of any of the above remedies, James may also claim compensation for losses he has suffered as a direct result of the defect – provided the dealer is at fault (or deemed at fault). In practice, dealers selling vehicles with latent manufacturing defects are routinely held to the fault standard under § 280 BGB. James's additional losses might include:

  • The cost of hiring a replacement vehicle while the BMW is off the road for diagnosis and repair
  • Expert or inspection fees paid to the UK specialist to diagnose and document the defect
  • Any reasonable travel costs incurred in arranging the remediation
  • In more serious cases – for example, where the defect caused a breakdown on the motorway – breakdown and recovery costs

How to assert your rights: step by step

1
Document the defect thoroughly and immediately As soon as you discover a fault, create a clear written record. Take photographs and video. Obtain a written diagnosis from an independent expert (for vehicles, a qualified mechanic or marque specialist). Note the date you first observed the problem, the mileage, and the usage conditions. This evidence is crucial, especially if the defect only appears after the first twelve-month presumption period.
2
Contact the seller in writing (Mängelrüge) Send a formal written notice of defect to the seller – by email (with read receipt) or registered post. State clearly: the nature of the defect, when it appeared, and the remedy you are demanding. For most cases, start with a demand for repair or replacement (Nacherfüllung). Set a reasonable deadline of 14–21 days. Keep copies of everything. Do not rely solely on telephone conversations.
3
Allow a reasonable deadline for the seller to respond Give the seller a fair opportunity to remedy the defect. For most goods, 14 days is standard. For complex goods requiring specialised parts (for example, a car awaiting a specific transmission component), a longer period may be appropriate. Document every communication and any repair attempts, including dates, what was done, and whether the defect recurred.
4
Escalate if the seller is uncooperative If the seller refuses to respond, denies the defect, or fails to repair it properly within the deadline, you move to the stronger remedies: withdrawal (Rücktritt) or price reduction (Minderung), potentially combined with a damages claim. Send a second formal letter specifying which remedy you are now invoking and another clear deadline. At this point, professional assistance – particularly where the seller is in Germany and you are abroad – becomes important.
5
Pursue the claim through German legal channels if necessary If the seller continues to refuse, the dispute must be resolved before the German civil courts. The court of first instance is determined by the claim value: the Amtsgericht (local court) for claims up to €5,000, the Landgericht (regional court) for higher amounts. For a €28,000 car purchase, the Landgericht would have jurisdiction, and legal representation before that court is mandatory. German civil procedure follows a "loser pays" costs rule, which means a well-founded claim can include recovery of legal costs.

Special considerations for cross-border purchases

If you are UK-based and have purchased goods from a German seller, there are some important cross-border points to bear in mind:

  • German law governs: A contract concluded with a German seller in Germany will almost always be governed by German law, and a consumer buying from a German trader retains the protection of German consumer law even if the contract contains a jurisdiction clause. This is confirmed by Article 6 of the Rome I Regulation, which continues to apply in the UK as retained EU law.
  • German courts have jurisdiction: For disputes with a German seller, proceedings will generally need to be brought before German courts (or the seller can choose to bring them in the UK, but rarely will). You do not need to be physically present in Germany to pursue a claim – a German legal adviser can represent you.
  • Time limits still apply: The two-year warranty period runs from delivery, regardless of where you are. If you bought goods from Germany and are now in the UK, the clock is the same. Do not delay in asserting your rights.
  • Private sellers versus traders: The full statutory protection described in this article applies when buying from a trader (a business). If you buy from a private individual in Germany, the warranty period can be reduced or even excluded by agreement. However, even a private seller cannot exclude liability for defects they deliberately concealed – doing so is fraudulent and gives rise to separate claims under § 123 BGB (deception).

What if the seller claims the defect is your fault?

A common response from sellers is to claim that the defect arose from the buyer's misuse, improper storage, or failure to maintain the goods correctly. Within the first twelve months, this argument faces a high evidentiary bar: the seller must prove misuse, not merely assert it. Beyond twelve months, the buyer must demonstrate the defect was pre-existing. In both cases, an independent expert report prepared shortly after the defect appeared is the single most important piece of evidence you can have.

Where a seller commissions their own expert who reaches a convenient conclusion, the courts can be asked to appoint a court-neutral expert (gerichtlicher Sachverständiger) to examine the goods and provide an independent opinion. In disputes over high-value items such as vehicles, this is common practice.

Compensation for losses beyond the purchase price

The remedies described above deal with the defective item itself. But a defective product often causes wider losses. Under §§ 280–281 BGB, a seller who is at fault (or whose fault is presumed) must compensate the buyer for all losses directly caused by the defect – what German law calls Schadensersatz statt der Leistung (damages in lieu of performance) and Schadensersatz neben der Leistung (damages alongside performance).

In the car example, if James's BMW broke down on a motorway because of the transmission fault, his consequential losses could extend to:

  • Breakdown and recovery costs (potentially several hundred euros)
  • Car hire for the period during which the car is off the road
  • Loss of earnings if the breakdown prevented him from attending a business appointment
  • Damage to other property caused by the defect (for example, if a defective tyre blew out and caused damage)
  • Diagnostic and expert report costs paid to establish the defect

These claims are separate from the main warranty claim and must be specifically documented and asserted. Keeping receipts and records of all losses from the moment a defect is discovered is essential.

Frequently asked questions

I bought a car from a German dealer six months ago. It's developed a serious fault. Do I have to go back to Germany?
Not necessarily. You can assert your rights in writing from the UK and should do so immediately. Within the first twelve months, the law presumes the defect was present at delivery – the dealer bears the burden of proving otherwise. A written demand by email or registered post to the German dealer, supported by an independent expert report, is the correct first step. If the dealer is uncooperative, a German legal adviser can pursue the claim on your behalf without you needing to travel.
The dealer says the warranty has expired because I'm outside Germany. Is that right?
No. The statutory warranty period under German law runs from delivery and applies regardless of where the buyer is located. A dealer cannot contractually restrict or nullify statutory warranty rights in a consumer transaction by reference to the buyer's location. If a seller attempts to do this, it may itself be an unfair contract term under § 307 BGB, rendering that term void.
The seller says I need to pay for the repair first and then claim it back. Is that correct?
Generally, no. Under Nacherfüllung, the seller must bear the costs directly – the buyer should not have to pay and recover. However, in some practical situations where the seller is in Germany and the goods are in the UK, there may need to be a pragmatic arrangement for how the repair is handled. If you pay for a repair that was clearly the seller's responsibility, you can claim reimbursement. Keep all invoices and document that you are paying under protest, reserving your rights to full reimbursement.
Can I claim compensation for the depreciation of a car that was delivered with a hidden defect?
Yes, in principle. If a vehicle is delivered with a defect that was not disclosed and that defect has reduced the car's market value (for example, hidden accident damage that affects its accident-free history), you can claim the difference in value as damages. This requires an expert valuation of the car both as delivered and as it would have been worth had it conformed to the contract. These cases are fact-specific and often require professional assistance to quantify correctly.
What is the difference between a warranty (Garantie) and the statutory Gewährleistung?
These are two different things. Gewährleistung (statutory warranty) is the mandatory legal protection given to every buyer under the BGB – it cannot be excluded in consumer contracts. Garantie (manufacturer's or dealer's guarantee) is a voluntary commercial promise, often offered by the manufacturer or a dealer in addition to the statutory rights. A guarantee may offer additional protection (for example, a three-year manufacturer's warranty) but can also have exclusions and conditions attached. Crucially, the existence of a manufacturer's guarantee does not in any way reduce your statutory Gewährleistung rights against the seller.
I bought the goods from a private seller in Germany, not a business. Do I have the same rights?
Your rights against a private seller are more limited. The full statutory warranty protection applies to contracts between consumers and traders. A private seller (Privatverkauf) can validly exclude warranty liability in the contract (and most private sales include the phrase "gekauft wie gesehen" – sold as seen). However, even a private seller cannot exclude liability for defects they deliberately concealed. If a private seller knew about a serious fault and hid it from you, that constitutes fraudulent misrepresentation (arglistige Täuschung) under § 123 BGB and gives you the right to rescind the contract.
